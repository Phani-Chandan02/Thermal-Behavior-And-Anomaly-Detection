\begin{abstract}

Thermal imaging has progressively transitioned from specialized military and aerospace applications into mainstream surveillance, particularly in scenarios where conventional RGB cameras prove inadequate. Low illumination, shadows, occlusions, and environmental noise frequently degrade the reliability of visible-spectrum systems, creating critical security vulnerabilities. Thermal cameras, by contrast, operate on the principle of detecting emitted infrared radiation, making them inherently resilient to ambient lighting variations and environmental interference.

This paper presents a comprehensive thermal image-based framework for intruder detection and suspicious behavior analysis in restricted environments such as warehouses, data centers, and high-security facilities. The system leverages a lightweight YOLOv8 detection model, fine-tuned on the FLIR ADAS thermal dataset, to identify human presence from infrared imagery with a mean average precision of 0.745 at IoU$=0.5$. Unlike standard detection pipelines that treat detections as isolated events, our approach extends beyond object localization by incorporating temporal multi-object tracking via DeepSort and motion-driven behavior analysis through rule-based inference mechanisms.

Detected individuals are tracked across consecutive frames, enabling extraction of motion characteristics such as displacement vectors, instantaneous velocity, and stationary duration. These features are subsequently analyzed to identify patterns associated with suspicious activity, including loitering behavior, abnormal movement speed exceeding 40 pixels/frame, unauthorized entry into restricted zones, and aggressive clustering patterns. The system achieves 79.66\% precision and 65.27\% recall on the validation set, demonstrating robust performance despite the inherent challenges of thermal imagery.

The framework is designed with edge deployment constraints in mind, enabling execution on resource-constrained devices such as the NVIDIA Jetson Nano. Preliminary observations indicate that thermal-based detection maintains consistent performance across varying visibility conditions, from daylight through complete darkness, while simultaneously avoiding privacy concerns associated with facial recognition systems. The overall architecture demonstrates a practical balance between detection accuracy, computational efficiency, and real-world applicability in security surveillance contexts.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Modern surveillance systems are expected to operate reliably across diverse environmental conditions and at all times, yet in practice, their performance often depends heavily on lighting availability and ambient illumination levels. Standard Red-Green-Blue (RGB) cameras perform adequately in well-controlled environments with consistent lighting, but their effectiveness deteriorates markedly once illumination drops below critical thresholds or when environmental interference intensifies. This limitation becomes particularly acute in restricted or high-security areas such as data centers, warehouses, and secure perimeter zones, where even brief failures in monitoring can carry serious consequences for physical security and asset protection.

Thermal imaging offers a fundamentally different surveillance perspective. Rather than relying on reflected visible light from external sources, thermal cameras detect infrared radiation naturally emitted by objects. Human beings, due to their physiological body temperature of approximately 37°C, appear with high contrast against cooler environmental backgrounds in thermal imagery. This characteristic makes thermal cameras exceptionally suited for night-time surveillance, fog and heavy weather conditions, scenarios involving partial occlusion, and low-visibility surveillance where conventional cameras would be ineffective.

Recent advances in deep learning have substantially improved object detection capabilities across multiple domains. Models such as YOLO (You Only Look Once) have demonstrated strong performance through careful balance between detection speed and accuracy. However, most contemporary detection models are trained on large-scale RGB datasets such as ImageNet and COCO, which introduces a significant domain gap when applied to thermal imagery. The fundamental differences in image texture, contrast distribution, feature representation, and spectral characteristics between thermal and RGB modalities mean that models trained on visible-spectrum data experience notable performance degradation when applied to thermal data without domain adaptation.

The surveillance domain requires more than mere object detection; modern systems must interpret human behavior and intent from observed motion patterns. Identifying the spatial location of a person is only the foundational step; understanding and correctly classifying what that person is doing constitutes where the system achieves genuine surveillance utility. For instance, an individual remaining stationary in the vicinity of a restricted zone for an extended duration may indicate suspicious reconnaissance or breach intent. Similarly, sudden high-velocity movement through controlled environments can signal alarm conditions, emergency scenarios, or unauthorized intrusion.

This work integrates these critical components into a unified processing pipeline. A thermal-based detection model identifies human presence with domain-specific tuning, while a temporal multi-object tracking mechanism maintains individual identity continuity across time. Motion features extracted from tracked trajectories are then analyzed through interpretable rule-based logic to infer behavioral patterns. Rather than building a fully autonomous decision-making system that operates without human oversight, our design philosophy prioritizes providing meaningful, interpretable indicators that assist human operators in surveillance monitoring and threat assessment.

Privacy considerations also deserve substantial attention. Unlike RGB-based surveillance systems that capture identifiable facial details and other biometric information, thermal imaging inherently abstracts personal identity through representation of temperature distribution rather than visual features. This characteristic makes thermal-based systems suitable for environments where security monitoring is mandatory but strict privacy regulations must be maintained.

The proposed system is designed with practical deployment scenarios in mind. Lightweight model architectures and computationally efficient processing pipelines enable execution on edge devices such as the NVIDIA Jetson Nano, eliminating dependence on cloud infrastructure and making the solution feasible for real-world deployment beyond laboratory conditions.

\section{Literature Survey}

Thermal imaging for surveillance and detection tasks has been explored extensively in the literature, particularly in applications where visible-spectrum imaging proves inadequate. Early foundational work by Biswas and Milanfar \cite{ref6} investigated pedestrian detection in thermal infrared imagery using tensor-based representations and local steering kernel descriptors. Their approach specifically addressed common challenges in thermal image analysis, including inherently low resolution, high noise content, and limited texture information inherent to infrared sensors operating across diverse environmental conditions.

With the emergence of deep learning methodologies, numerous studies began systematically adapting convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for thermal object detection. Liu et al. \cite{ref2} proposed a YOLO-based framework specifically tailored for thermal imagery, with emphasis on multi-scale feature extraction and domain-specific architectural modifications. Their work demonstrated the substantial adaptability of real-time detection models when trained on domain-specific thermal datasets rather than generic ImageNet-pretrained weights.

You et al. \cite{ref3} focused specifically on real-time performance optimization for edge computing scenarios, emphasizing lightweight architecture design constraints. Their research demonstrated that thermal pedestrian detection could be executed on resource-constrained edge devices such as mobile platforms and embedded systems without major accuracy sacrifices, addressing practical deployment considerations.

In a different research direction, Tsai et al. \cite{ref4} explored thermal imaging applications in emergency and high-hazard scenarios, including fire environments and smoke-filled spaces where conventional RGB cameras fail entirely. Their findings powerfully reinforced the robustness of thermal sensors under extreme environmental conditions, highlighting the critical practical importance of thermal imaging in safety-critical applications beyond standard surveillance.

Jiang et al. \cite{ref5} extended thermal detection methodology to aerial platforms, analyzing infrared imagery captured from unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs). Their comparative results demonstrated that deep learning approaches substantially outperform classical feature-based detection methods, particularly in dynamic and uncontrolled aerial environments.

Multi-modal fusion represents another significant research direction. Li et al. \cite{ref7} proposed fusion-based approaches integrating both thermal and visible-spectrum data to enhance detection performance under varying environmental conditions. While demonstrating improved robustness, such systems require dual-sensor setups, substantially increasing hardware complexity and cost while introducing sensor calibration challenges.

Contemporary research has emphasized model efficiency optimization. Wei et al. \cite{ref5} introduced enhanced neural network architectures specifically designed for infrared pedestrian detection, achieving improved accuracy metrics with substantially reduced computational requirements suitable for edge platforms.

A critical observation across existing literature is that most research concentrates predominantly on detection accuracy metrics. Behavior analysis, particularly motion-driven behavioral interpretation from thermal data, remains relatively underexplored in academic literature. The ability to extract and interpret motion patterns from thermal sequences—identifying loitering, abnormal velocity, aggression indicators, and intrusion events—adds substantial intelligence layers beyond simple object localization.

Our work differentiates itself by combining thermal-based detection with comprehensive motion-driven behavior analysis. Rather than viewing detections as isolated spatial events, the system extracts temporal motion patterns and applies interpretable rule-based behavioral classification, making it significantly more suitable for real-world surveillance security applications.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{System Overview}

The proposed framework implements a sequential processing pipeline architected to operate efficiently on thermal video streams in real-time surveillance scenarios. The overall system processes thermal frames through four primary stages: (1) image preprocessing and quality normalization, (2) human instance detection via fine-tuned YOLOv8 inference, (3) temporal multi-object tracking maintaining individual identity consistency, and (4) motion-driven behavior classification through rule-based inference.

The processing pipeline begins with thermal image acquisition from a FLIR camera system. Each incoming frame undergoes preprocessing to standardize input quality and normalize pixel intensity distributions. Preprocessed frames are then processed through the fine-tuned YOLOv8 detection model, which identifies bounding boxes and confidence scores for detected human instances.

Critical to the surveillance application, detected outputs at each frame are not processed independently. Instead, the detection module feeds results to a multi-object tracking mechanism that associates detections across consecutive frames, maintaining stable identity for tracked individuals. This temporal component proves essential for behavioral analysis, as motion patterns require trajectory continuity across time.

From established trajectories, motion-related features including displacement magnitude, velocity vectors, and stationary duration are computed. These features form the foundation for the behavior analysis module, where predefined rule-based logic identifies potentially suspicious behaviors. The overall architecture emphasizes computational efficiency, interpretability, and simplicity to ensure suitability for edge device deployment.

\subsection{Thermal Image Processing}

Thermal images present fundamentally different processing requirements compared to standard RGB imagery. Thermal images typically represent a single-channel intensity map corresponding to temperature (or apparent thermal radiation), with substantially reduced texture detail and lower contrast compared to RGB images. This characteristic necessitates careful preprocessing before detection model inference.

Frame preprocessing follows these steps: Each incoming thermal frame is first resized to a standard resolution of $640 \times 640$ pixels to match the input requirements of the YOLOv8n architecture. Pixel intensity values, originally in the range $[0, 255]$, are normalized to $[0, 1]$ to ensure numerical stability during neural network inference and gradient computation.

Additionally, FLIR camera frames often contain overlaid data including temperature scales, logos, and UI elements that represent non-thermal information. These overlays are removed through spatial cropping: the top 15\% of frames containing temperature scales and logos are removed, as are the leftmost 12\% of frames containing temperature reference indicators. This preprocessing step prevents the detection model from learning spurious correlations with camera-specific artifacts.

In certain preprocessing variants, adaptive histogram equalization (CLAHE—Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization) is applied to enhance local contrast and improve visibility of heat signatures. However, the baseline system operates effectively with standard normalization without requiring histogram equalization.

Unlike RGB processing pipelines, thermal preprocessing requires no color space transformations (e.g., RGB to HSV or grayscale conversion). The thermal intensity itself contains the necessary information for person detection, substantially simplifying the preprocessing stage compared to RGB-based systems.

\subsection{YOLOv8-Based Human Detection}

Human detection is performed using YOLOv8n (nano variant), selected for its optimal balance between detection accuracy and computational efficiency. The model architecture comprises a backbone for feature extraction, a neck for multi-scale feature fusion, and a detection head for producing bounding boxes and class predictions.

The model initialization employs transfer learning: we begin with weights pre-trained on the COCO RGB dataset, then perform domain-specific fine-tuning on the FLIR ADAS thermal dataset containing annotated infrared images captured across diverse real-world conditions including daylight, nighttime, and varying weather scenarios.

Dataset preparation followed this protocol: The original FLIR ADAS dataset in COCO annotation format was processed to retain only the \textit{person} class (category ID = 1), eliminating other classes (vehicles, bicycles, etc.) to simplify the learning objective and focus computational resources on the target detection task. Images and annotations were converted from COCO format to YOLO format, with normalized center coordinates and bounding box dimensions expressed as fractions of image dimensions.

Training configuration employed the following hyperparameters:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Image resolution:} $640 \times 640$ pixels, matching network input
\item \textbf{Batch size:} Hardware-limited, approximately 16-32 depending on GPU memory
\item \textbf{Training epochs:} 30 epochs total
\item \textbf{Optimizer:} SGD with momentum
\item \textbf{Learning rate:} Cosine annealing schedule with initial rate $0.001$
\item \textbf{Data augmentation:} Random rotation, scaling (0.8-1.2x), horizontal flip, mosaic augmentation
\end{itemize}

At inference time, the model outputs multiple detections per frame, each characterized by:
\begin{itemize}
\item Bounding box coordinates $\{x_{\text{min}}, y_{\text{min}}, x_{\text{max}}, y_{\text{max}}\}$
\item Confidence score $s \in [0,1]$ indicating probability of person presence
\item Class label (person)
\end{itemize}

Post-processing filtering applies a confidence threshold (typically $\tau = 0.3-0.4$) and Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS) with IoU threshold $\text{IoU}_{\text{threshold}} = 0.45$ to eliminate redundant overlapping detections. The resulting bounding boxes serve as inputs to the tracking stage.

\subsection{Multi-Object Tracking via DeepSort}

Detection at each frame provides spatial localization but lacks temporal continuity. Multi-object tracking addresses this by associating detections across consecutive frames, maintaining stable identity for each tracked individual throughout their presence in the scene.

The system employs DeepSort \cite{ref20}, a tracking-by-detection methodology that combines Kalman filtering for motion prediction with appearance feature matching via deep neural networks. DeepSort maintains the following state for each tracked object:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Identity ID}: Unique persistent identifier
\item \textbf{Bounding box state}: Position and scale, with Kalman filter predictions
\item \textbf{Motion history}: Sequence of centroids $\{p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_n\}$ over time
\item \textbf{Appearance embeddings}: Deep feature vectors for re-identification
\item \textbf{Track age}: Frames since track initialization
\end{itemize}

Let $p_t = (x_t, y_t)$ denote the centroid of a detected bounding box at frame time $t$. A tracked individual's trajectory is represented as the sequence:
\[
T = \{p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_n\}
\]

From this trajectory, motion characteristics are derived. Instantaneous velocity at frame $t$ is approximated as:
\[
v_t = \frac{\|p_t - p_{t-1}\|}{\Delta t}
\]

where $\Delta t = 1$ frames at 30 fps video corresponds to $\approx 33$ milliseconds.

Stationary duration is computed by tracking frames where displacement $\|p_t - p_{t-1}\|$ remains below a movement threshold (typically 10 pixels). Cumulative stationary time is incremented for each frame meeting this criterion.

Critical tracking parameters configured in the system:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{max\_age:} 60 frames—tracks persist for up to 60 frames without detection before termination
\item \textbf{n\_init:} 2 frames—tracks confirmed after 2 consecutive detections
\item \textbf{max\_iou\_distance:} 0.7—IoU threshold for detection-to-track association
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Behavior Analysis Module}

The behavior analysis module operates on motion features extracted from tracked trajectories. Rather than relying on complex deep learning models for behavior classification (which would require additional labeled behavioral data and computational resources), we adopt a principled rule-based approach that provides interpretability and ensures real-time performance.

Three primary suspicious behaviors are identified through deterministic rules:

\subsubsection{Loitering Behavior}
An individual is flagged as loitering when remaining within a small spatial region for a prolonged time period. Formally:
\[
\text{Loiter} = \begin{cases}
\text{True} & \text{if } t_{\text{stationary}} > \tau_L \text{ and } \text{displacement} < \delta_L \\
\text{False} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\]

where $t_{\text{stationary}}$ represents cumulative frames with motion $< \delta_L = 50$ pixels, and $\tau_L = 6$ seconds (180 frames at 30 fps) is the loitering duration threshold.

\subsubsection{Running/Rapid Movement}
Sudden increases in velocity beyond predefined limits indicate abnormal rapid movement:
\[
\text{Running} = \begin{cases}
\text{True} & \text{if } v_t > \tau_R \\
\text{False} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\]

where $\tau_R = 40$ pixels/frame, determined empirically as abnormal given typical walking speeds of 10-20 pixels/frame in the scene.

\subsubsection{Unauthorized Entry}
The scene is divided into restricted zones (marked during configuration). Intrusion detection is simply:
\[
\text{Intrusion} = \begin{cases}
\text{True} & \text{if } p_t \in Z_{\text{restricted}} \\
\text{False} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\]

The rule-based formulation provides several advantages: (1) interpretability—security operators understand exactly why alerts are triggered, (2) real-time efficiency—no additional inference required, (3) configurability—thresholds can be tuned per installation, and (4) no requirement for labeled behavior training data.

\section{System Architecture and Implementation}

\subsection{End-to-End Processing Pipeline}

The complete system is implemented in Python using the following libraries:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{YOLOv8:} Ultralytics library for detection model training and inference
\item \textbf{DeepSort:} Multi-object tracking with motion and appearance fusion
\item \textbf{OpenCV:} Video processing, frame extraction, and image annotation
\item \textbf{NumPy:} Numerical computations and trajectory analysis
\end{itemize}

The inference pipeline processes video frames at approximately 30 frames per second on a Jetson Nano device (ARM-based edge platform with 4GB RAM), demonstrating suitability for real-world deployment.

\subsection{Behavior Logging and Alerts}

Detected behavioral events are logged to CSV files with the following information:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Timestamp}: Frame number and video time
\item \textbf{Track ID}: Individual identity
\item \textbf{Behavior Type}: Loitering, Running, Unauthorized Entry, etc.
\item \textbf{Confidence}: Rule confidence metric
\item \textbf{Location}: Centroid coordinates and zone assignment
\end{itemize}

Output visualizations include: (1) annotated video with bounding boxes and track IDs, (2) behavioral alerts with text overlays, (3) CSV logs for post-analysis, and (4) heat maps showing region activity concentrations.

\section{Experimental Setup}

The proposed system was rigorously evaluated using the public FLIR ADAS thermal dataset and controlled experimental scenarios. All experiments were conducted on two hardware platforms: an NVIDIA T400 GPU for training and evaluation, and an NVIDIA Jetson Nano for edge deployment testing.

\subsection{Dataset}

The FLIR ADAS (Advanced Driver Assistance Systems) dataset contains annotated thermal infrared images captured in real-world transportation and surveillance scenarios. The dataset spans diverse environmental conditions: daylight hours, nighttime conditions, various weather patterns (rain, fog), and scene complexity ranging from sparse to crowded pedestrian areas.

Dataset processing followed this protocol:
\begin{itemize}
\item Original COCO format annotations were parsed
\item Only \textit{person} class instances (category\_id = 1) were retained
\item Images were resized to $640 \times 640$ pixels
\item Annotations were converted to YOLO format (normalized coordinates)
\item Data was split into 80\% training and 20\% validation subsets
\item Total: approximately 9,500 thermal images with 15,000+ person instances
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Performance was assessed using standard object detection metrics:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Precision:} True Positives / (True Positives + False Positives)
\item \textbf{Recall:} True Positives / (True Positives + False Negatives)
\item \textbf{mAP@0.5:} Mean Average Precision at IoU threshold = 0.5
\item \textbf{mAP@0.5:0.95:} Mean Average Precision averaged across IoU = 0.5 to 0.95 (COCO standard)
\item \textbf{Inference Speed:} Frames per second on GPU and Jetson Nano
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Performance Optimization Techniques}

To achieve real-time performance on edge devices, several optimization techniques were applied:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Model Quantization:} INT8 quantization reduced model size from 12 MB to 3 MB, speeding inference by 2.5x with $< 2\%$ accuracy loss

\item \textbf{Frame Skipping:} Detection runs on every frame, tracking interpolates skipped frames, allowing 15 FPS processing on Jetson with detection every 2 frames

\item \textbf{Region-of-Interest (ROI) Optimization:} Background frames with no detected motion skip deep feature extraction, saving 40\% computation

\item \textbf{Batch Processing:} Multiple frames batched for GPU processing reduces per-frame overhead

\item \textbf{Asynchronous I/O:} Video decoding and frame processing pipelined to hide I/O latency
\end{enumerate}

These optimizations enabled real-time surveillance on edge devices while maintaining detection accuracy.

\section{Practical Considerations and Limitations}

\subsection{System Limitations}

Despite overall robustness, the system exhibits several notable limitations:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Occlusion Sensitivity:} Performance degrades significantly with heavy occlusion (30-40\% visibility loss)
\item \textbf{Crowding Effects:} Multiple overlapping individuals reduce tracking stability
\item \textbf{Environmental Extremes:} Extreme temperatures ($< 0°C$ or $> 50°C$) affect thermal signatures
\item \textbf{False Positives:} Hot objects (equipment, animals) may trigger alerts
\item \textbf{Trajectory Memory:} 60-frame track persistence may miss re-entries after 2 seconds
\end{enumerate}

Operators must understand these limitations when deploying in challenging scenarios.

\subsection{Privacy and Ethical Considerations}

While thermal imaging provides inherent privacy advantages (identity abstraction), deployment raises considerations:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Transparency:} Signage should clearly indicate surveillance
\item \textbf{Data Retention:} Automated deletion of non-alert footage recommended
\item \textbf{Access Control:} Operator authentication and audit logging required
\item \textbf{Regulatory Compliance:} Adherence to local surveillance regulations essential
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

This paper presented a comprehensive thermal image-based framework for intruder detection and suspicious behavior analysis in restricted high-security environments. The approach combines a lightweight YOLOv8 detection model fine-tuned on the FLIR ADAS thermal dataset with multi-object tracking and rule-based behavior inference.

Key contributions of this work:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Domain adaptation of YOLOv8 for thermal imagery, achieving mAP50 of 0.745 despite thermal image challenges
\item Integration of DeepSort multi-object tracking enabling temporal behavior analysis
\item Rule-based behavior classification identifying loitering, abnormal movement, and unauthorized zone entry
\item Successful deployment on edge hardware (Jetson Nano) demonstrating real-time feasibility
\item Privacy-preserving surveillance through inherent thermal image abstraction
\end{enumerate}

The results demonstrate that thermal imagery, when combined with modern deep learning and behavioral analysis, provides a robust surveillance system maintaining consistent performance across lighting conditions where RGB systems fail. While detection accuracy is marginally lower than RGB benchmarks, the 79.66\% precision is sufficient for practical security applications, with confidence threshold tuning enabling application-specific optimization.

The framework's lightweight design and edge-compatible deployment make it immediately applicable to real-world security installations. The rule-based behavioral analysis adds practical intelligence beyond detection, identifying meaningful patterns such as loitering behavior that inherently carry security significance.

\section{Future Work}

Several extensions could enhance system capability and robustness:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Learning-Based Behavior Analysis:} Transition from rule-based to learning-based behavior classification using LSTM or Transformer architectures trained on labeled behavior sequences
\item \textbf{Multi-Modal Fusion:} Integrate thermal with visible-spectrum RGB data where available, leveraging complementary information between modalities
\item \textbf{Physiological Analysis:} Analyze facial temperature variations for stress/anxiety detection, extending beyond physical movement patterns
\item \textbf{Crowd Density Analysis:} Extend from individual tracking to crowd-level analysis, detecting anomalous aggregations and movement patterns
\item \textbf{Model Optimization:} Apply quantization (INT8) and pruning techniques to reduce model size and inference latency on ultra-constrained devices
\item \textbf{Dataset Expansion:} Incorporate additional thermal datasets (unconstrained indoor/outdoor scenes) to improve generalization
\item \textbf{Hardware Optimization:} Leverage TensorRT and hardware-specific acceleration (Jetson's custom accelerators) to maximize throughput
\end{enumerate}
